Participant Attitudes Toward AI Interviews

Insights from 13 participants on perceived similarity to human researchers, situational preferences, and future participation intent

Project Overview

An analysis exploring how people who participated in AI-conducted 1-on-1 interviews experienced them — how similar the AI felt to a human researcher, in what situations they prefer AI interviews, and their intent to participate again. Responses from 13 participants across various research topics were synthesized into a unified view of attitudes, preferences, and improvement requests.

report_problem

Research Objective

Comprehensively understand overall AI interview experience ratings, perceived similarity to a human researcher (1–10 scale), human vs. AI preference, reasons and situations for preferring AI, and future participation intent.

person

Participants

Adults who had experienced at least one AI interview: 13 participants total — 5 from perception/lifestyle research topics, 8 from local government event participation research and others.

analytics

Topics Covered & Deliverables

10-point human-likeness score distribution, preference choice (human vs. AI), reasons and situations for preferring AI, overall experience, future participation intent, and implications and recommendations.

Research Methodology

smart_toy

AI Interview (Proby)

Participants engaged in 1-on-1 AI interviews via Proby. At the close of each interview, a shared question block assessed attitudes toward the AI interview experience — covering overall experience, 10-point human-likeness rating, human vs. AI preference, reasons/situations for preferring AI, and future participation intent. This block was extracted from 13 interviews and analyzed in aggregate. No additional stimuli were used — purely conversational.

Key Findings

"The response-and-follow-up happened so fluidly, and the questions felt tailored to each moment — I honestly thought it was almost perfect."

— Participant

1. Perceived Similarity to a Human Researcher (10-point scale)

Average scores clustered around 7 points. Score distribution: 10 points (1 person), 8 points (3), 7 points (6), 6–6.5 points (2), "feels human" without a specific score (1). Key reasons for lower scores: (1) Tone feeling stiff or mechanical, (2) noticeable delay between receiving an answer and the next question, (3) occasional failures to understand speech or a sense of being misheard, (4) lack of a real-time conversational back-and-forth feel, (5) impression that genuine empathetic responses were lacking.

2. Preference: Human vs. AI Interview

"Would always choose human" — 1 participant (~8%). However, this participant also said they would consider AI when time and location aren't a constraint, and expressed intent to participate again. "AI interviews would be preferred in some situations" — 12 participants (~92%). Conditional or situational preference for AI interviews was overwhelming.

3. When and Why Participants Prefer AI Interviews

Reduced pressure: Human interviews can feel pressurizing, but AI waits without rushing — making it easier to respond comfortably. No bias or judgment. Time and location flexibility. Honesty and sensitive topics: Private or sensitive topics felt easier to answer honestly with AI. Technical convenience. Topic/purpose fit: Simple factual questions or pre-defined structured responses were cited as AI-appropriate.

"Because it's AI, it waits even when I answer slowly — I can respond without any pressure, which I really liked." — Participant

4. Overall Experience & Future Participation Intent

Positive expressions included: "it felt like an actual conversation," "it asked great follow-up questions based on my answers," "almost like talking to a real person," "it felt like a real interview," and "it was a new kind of experience I enjoyed." Improvement requests focused on making the tone softer and more natural, and reducing interruptions. All 13 participants expressed intent to participate in AI interviews again — with responses including "absolutely," "I'll join anytime," and "I'd actively participate."

Strengths (Maintain & Amplify)

12 of 13 participants responded closely to "AI interviews are preferred in some situations," and the relative advantages of AI interviews were recognized across: reduced pressure, absence of bias, time flexibility, honesty with sensitive topics, and technical convenience. All 13 expressed future participation intent. The 7-point average signals that with "just better tone" or "just better speech recognition," many participants feel the AI could approach human-level quality.

Improvement Areas

1. Tone & Naturalness

Reduce stiffness, formality, and mechanical feel. "Softer, more natural, without interruptions" was a repeatedly cited request.

2. Response-to-Question Latency

The perceived delay between receiving an answer and delivering the next question affects the sense of real-time conversation. Explore near-real-time conversational design.

3. Speech & Language Understanding

Improving accuracy in understanding accents, dialects, and contextual meaning directly translates to higher perceived similarity scores and overall satisfaction.

4. Empathetic Responsiveness

Strengthening emotional and experiential reflection — "genuine empathy" and "responses that draw out experience" — offers the potential to push average scores above 8.

Deployment Direction

AI interviews can be confidently recommended as the primary format for "simple Q&A," "objective or sensitive topics," and "cases where time and location flexibility matter most." Even the one participant who "would always choose human" was open to AI under convenient conditions — suggesting that human researchers can be reserved for deep/emotional sections, while AI handles straightforward/structured segments. This hybrid model should inform future research design.

One-line summary: Participants broadly accept AI interviews and recognize their advantages around pressure reduction, absence of bias, flexibility, honesty with sensitive topics, and technical convenience. Addressing tone, latency, speech recognition, and empathetic responsiveness will further increase human-likeness scores and satisfaction — and the topic/situation-based human + AI hybrid design approach can be directly applied to future research projects.
