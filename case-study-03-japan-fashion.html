Preferred Fashion App UI Direction for Japanese Users

When price, coupons, and reviews are visible at a glance — and navigation is one tap away — Japanese users feel the app respects their time

Project Overview

When designing apps for the Japanese market (e-commerce, fashion, retail), this project aimed to consolidate "what Japanese users actually said they prefer" — so design direction could be grounded in user evidence rather than assumption, and clearly communicated to teams and stakeholders.

report_problem

Research Objective

Identify the design direction and must-have attributes preferred by Japanese users. Collect per-screen UI version preferences (Home / List / Detail) along with detailed rationale.

person

Participants & Stimuli

Japan-based users. Three UI versions per screen (Home, Search Results List, Product Detail) were presented across both Korean and Japanese market fashion apps. Participants selected the most preferred version and provided detailed reasoning via AI in-depth interview.

palette

Deliverables

5 preferred design directions for Japanese users, per-screen preferred version and rationale, must-have attribute table, cultural/psychological factor analysis, and anti-patterns — formatted as a Product Designer reference document.

Research Methodology

smart_toy

AI In-Depth Interview (Proby)

Each participant began by sharing their general fashion app habits and preferences. The AI then presented three UI versions per screen (Home, List, Product Detail) and asked them to select their preferred version. Probing questions dug into the specific reasons for each choice. Final questions covered cross-screen design preferences and whether those preferences connect to Japanese cultural sensibilities.

UI stimuli (app screens) were sent in advance via email. During the interview, participants referenced them by screen name/number to identify their selections.

Key Findings

"When a site values my time, I want to keep browsing — I want to keep enjoying it."

— Japanese participant

1. Time-Respecting UX

Waiting, loading, unnecessary steps, and information overload all create a feeling of "my time is being wasted." Conversely, when users can reach the information they need in one step and menus/search open in one tap, the perception is "this app values my time" — directly translating to trust and return visits. CTAs and primary actions should be one-tap accessible; loading, transitions, and popups should be minimized; each screen should contain only scannable, necessary information.

"When there's no unnecessary time or information, and menus open in one tap with detailed search available — that means the site is respecting me."

2. Price & Promotion Clarity

Hidden or ambiguous prices and discounts trigger distrust — participants said it felt like the site was "trying to deceive." When the regular price, discounted price, tax, shipping, and applicable coupons are all understandable at a glance, users feel "this site is honest." Prices, discounts, and coupons should be visible and comparable within a single block across Home, List, and Detail views. Promo code input and the resulting calculation should be shown in context (Detail or Cart).

"When the price isn't clearly visible, I start to wonder — is this a sketchy site? Are they trying to hide the price?"

3. Shopping = Joy — Clean, Organized Design

Participants want shopping to be about "discovery and browsing," not just necessity purchasing. Colors and typography that feel too rigid can make shopping feel like work rather than pleasure. Users who use minimal brands like MUJI consistently equated "simple = trustworthy = preferred," and preferred app designs with a clear information hierarchy and no unnecessary elements. "Fun" is defined not by excessive decoration, but by clarity plus visual satisfaction.

"Shopping is supposed to be enjoyable. So fun colors and enjoyable typography really matter."

4. One-Screen Completeness

At each stage (Home / List / Detail), the critical question was: "Can I make my decision here without going somewhere else?" Coupons, prices, reviews, likes, filters, and cart scattered across multiple pages made users feel their time was being wasted. Interview results showed unanimous preference for QM1 (Home) — coupons, popular items, categories, search, and cart all in one view — JM2 (List) — toggle for sale/available-now items, menu, popularity, and reviews in one screen — and KM3 (Detail) — price, discount, tax, shipping, and quantity all in one block. The common thread: "one screen/one block resolves everything needed" = time respect + transparency.

5. Cultural & Emotional Factors: Consideration, Transparency, Trust, Negative Space

Preferences for "time respect," "price transparency," "single-screen resolution," and "simplicity" can be connected to recurring Japanese cultural sensibilities: Omotenashi (attentiveness): reducing friction proactively = saving clicks = feeling cared for. Clarity / ambiguity aversion: showing prices and conditions openly reduces anxiety and makes decisions easier. Trust = transparency: information that's openly presented creates the feeling of "this site is honest." Negative space / minimalism: too much visual noise signals disorganization and reduces trust.

6. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Hiding or minimizing price and discount information → triggers "suspicious" / "trying to deceive" responses. Distributing needed information across multiple pages → "wasting my time," "this site doesn't value me." Excessive loading, popups, or multi-step flows → breaks immersion, causes drop-off. Ignoring brand identity (e.g., a "simple is our identity" brand like MUJI with a cluttered UI) → breaks trust immediately.

Japanese Cultural Context

Recurring consumer psychology from the interviews. Reflecting these sensibilities in UI design can create the impression that "this app understands me."

⏱️

Time Respect

A strong awareness that "there are only 24 hours" makes unnecessary waiting or steps feel like "stealing my time." One-tap access and minimized loading are read as genuine consideration.

🙏

Omotenashi (Attentiveness)

Anticipating the other person's needs and reducing friction. "Letting them reach what they need in one step" = saving clicks = the impression of being genuinely cared for.

✨

Clarity / Ambiguity Aversion

A preference for "clear" over "ambiguous." Showing prices, coupons, and conditions openly reduces anxiety and makes decisions easier.

💎

Trust = Transparency

Belief that trust builds when nothing is hidden. When price, discount, and terms are visible at a glance, the site feels "honest."

⬜

Negative Space / Minimalism

An aesthetic that values negative space and "only what's needed." Information overload or visual noise can signal disorganization and reduce trust.

🛡️

Psychological Safety

A desire to avoid anything that looks like a scam or uncertain choice. Clear prices and conditions create the feeling of having avoided a "bad purchase."

One-line summary: Japanese users' preferences (time respect, transparency, single-screen resolution, simplicity) are rooted in cultural sensibilities around consideration, clarity, trust, and negative space. When design reflects these values, it creates the impression that "this app values me and won't deceive me" — reducing drop-off and connecting to revisit and conversion.

Recommendations for Product Designers

Must-Have Attributes Summary

Price & Discount Visibility

Large text, color emphasis for discounted prices (red/yellow), displayed within the same block.

Coupon & Promo Exposure

Coupon area and promo code input visible on Home and Detail screens.

Reviews & Likes Count

Review count and likes displayed on List and Detail views — ideally on a single screen.

Filters & Toggles

Quick screening options like "sale only" or "in stock" — toggled from the top of the list.

Search & Menu Access

Reach search and deep menu navigation in one tap — persistent search bar and hamburger menu with consistent placement.

Time Respect

Minimize loading. One-tap access to CTAs and primary actions. Eliminate unnecessary popups and multi-step flows.

One-sentence summary: Japanese users say "I want to keep using this app" when price, coupons, and reviews are visible at a glance, navigation and search are one tap away, and there's no unnecessary information or steps — in other words, when they feel the app respects their time. Design should first embody that "time respect" and "transparency" visually and structurally.
