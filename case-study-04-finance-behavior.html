MZ Generation Financial Lifestyle & Banking Insights

A deep dive into financial interest, spending/saving/investing habits, bank usage, app experience, and brand perception — with digital marketing campaign recommendations

Case Study Sections

Project Overview

A comprehensive study to understand how the MZ generation (20s–40s) thinks about money, which banks they use, and how they respond to apps and advertising. Seven distinct financial life domains were covered in a single research effort, with results used to recommend digital marketing campaign directions targeting MZ consumers.

report_problem

Research Objective

Deeply understand how participants think about money and finances, how they spend/save/invest, how they choose and switch banks, their app experience, and their impressions of bank advertising — and use these findings to propose a digital marketing strategy direction.

person

Participants

7 participants aged 20–40 (mix of salaried employees and self-employed). Primary banks used: Shinhan, KB Kookmin, Nonghyup, Toss Bank, Woori, Hana, and others.

analytics

Topics Covered & Deliverables

① Financial interest & information sources ② Spending/saving/investing allocation and satisfaction ③ Branch visit reasons and friction ④ Primary bank selection, retention, and switching intent ⑤ Bank brand image and preference ⑥ Mobile banking app experience ⑦ Bank advertising recall and evaluation. Delivered a comprehensive analysis report and 6-domain digital marketing recommendations.

Research Methodology

smart_toy

AI Interview (Proby)

7 participants engaged in 1-on-1 AI-moderated interviews using Proby's platform. Based on questions about financial life and banking behavior, the AI generated contextual follow-up questions — resulting in average interview depths of approximately 24 minutes per participant.

Each participant's level of financial interest and banking experience varied considerably, so even identical questions yielded answers with different depths and specificity — capturing the genuine diversity of MZ financial behavior.

Key Findings

"These days I'm quite interested in stocks… I'm also practicing the basics — like splitting my savings accounts. I'm even taking financial literacy courses."

— Interview 5 (female, 40s, employed)

1. Financial Interest & Information Gathering: Investing, Saving, and Basics — via News, Apps, YouTube & Group Chats

All participants mentioned at least one of: investing (stocks, ETFs, crypto, pension savings), saving, and basic financial management (account splitting). "Salary alone isn't enough," "friends are actually making money from investments," and "there's a trend of trying to build wealth through investment rather than hard work" were recurring statements. Information channels ranged from Naver news, stock/banking apps, YouTube (finance influencers), group chats, books, and government financial literacy courses.

"Assets need to grow but salary alone has limits, and the stock market has been rising a lot lately." (female, 30s, employed)

"In my generation there's a strong tendency to try to make money through investment rather than working hard at a company." (female, 20s, employed)

2. Spending / Saving / Investing Allocation: Wide Variance, With Consistent Desire to "Save More" and "Invest More"

Spending ranged from 10–50%, investing from 0–40%, and saving from 10–90% — showing significant lifestyle-driven variation. Those who were dissatisfied said they wanted to "save more," "increase the investment portion," or "gave themselves a low score because I'm studying but not actually investing yet." Those who were satisfied said they were content with the current allocation but could see room to increase investments.

"I'd ideally want savings at 80%… but with a loan and other expenses, it doesn't seem easy right now." (female, 30s, employed)

3. Bank Branch Visits: Limited to Specific Needs, With Both Physical and Psychological Friction

Visit frequency ranged from "once or twice a year" to "every month or two." Reasons for visiting were narrow: product consultations (wanting direct explanation), transactions requiring in-person authentication, currency exchange, and VIP consultation room for multiple tasks. Physical friction: long wait and service times, app-based appointments not actually reducing wait. Psychological friction: website content written "without considering whether the reader has financial knowledge," and AI chatbots providing "formulaic, mechanical, lengthy responses" — pushing users to seek an actual human who can answer fluidly.

"Website product descriptions are very detailed, but they seem to be written without regard for whether the reader has financial knowledge. AI chatbots tend to give formal, mechanical, and lengthy answers — so I go to the branch to find someone who can answer organically." (female, 20s, employed)

4. Primary Bank Relationship: Formed Through Life Context, "Lazy but Would Switch for the Right Offer"

The most common reasons for a primary bank relationship forming were overwhelmingly life-context factors: parents' bank, nearest branch to home or school, student ID linkage, rural agricultural bank (Nonghyup), housing subscription, payroll account, or simply starting to use Toss and staying. Loyalty was built through coupons, VIP benefits, long-term customer rewards, branch/advisor availability, parking account interest rates, and payment point cashback. Switching intent was not "impossible" — the dominant response was "too lazy to switch, but I would if the offer was good enough."

"I've accumulated coupons and VIP membership perks from using it so long… those things make it hard to switch to another bank." (female, 30s, employed)

5. Bank Brand Perception: KB & Shinhan Lead; Hana Bank = "No Particular Image" / "Feels Old"

When asked to name their top 3 preferred banks, results showed: KB Kookmin (5 mentions), Shinhan (4), Woori (3), Toss Bank/Hana/Nonghyup (2 each). KB was remembered via "scale, yellow color, school connection, Kim Yuna ad." Shinhan via "young, trendy, game sponsorship." Woori via "e-sports, youthful, forex fee waivers." Hana Bank repeatedly scored "no particular image" and "feels old." Its preference mentions came only from long-term users citing "being treated well."

"KB and Hana both feel a bit old to me." (female, 30s, employed)

"Hana Bank doesn't really bring any particular image to mind." (female, 20s, employed)

6. Mobile Banking App Experience: Toss/Kakao = "Fast & Light"; Hana Q = "Clean & Secure, But Slow & Underutilized"

Toss and KakaoBank praised for: "fast and light," "frequently used features are immediately accessible," "minimal depth." Shinhan family criticized for: "cluttered menus," "app fragmented into multiple sub-apps." Hana Q saw mixed reviews: "clean, simple," "security alerts and spending tracking are great" alongside "slow loading," "app occasionally crashes," and "just average compared to Toss/Kakao." Notably, users of Hana Q reported not using it for financial information — "there doesn't seem to be anything special there, and the navigation feels inconvenient."

"KakaoBank and Toss are super fast and lightweight, and frequently used services come up without multiple taps. Hana Bank's app is neither very convenient nor very inconvenient — just average compared to Toss/Kakao." (female, 30s, self-employed)

7. Bank Ad Recall: Strong TV + Celebrity Recall; Hana Bank Body Weak; Hana Card Event Memorable

TV dominated awareness, with single scenes anchored to celebrities (Park Bo-gum, Kim Yuna, IU, G-Dragon, Son Heung-min) being strongly recalled. Hana Bank main brand ads drew mostly "nothing comes to mind" responses. Hana Q saw sparse recall of "forex exchange benefit," "Son Heung-min banner," and "G-Dragon's green hair." However, a Hana Card travel popup event (airplane/airport concept, passport-style ticket) was vividly and clearly remembered through SNS and direct experience.

"The Hana Card travel-related card popup event I saw on SNS stands out. They ran it like you were inside an airplane going through departure procedures, with an entry ticket that looked like a passport." (female, 30s, self-employed)

Digital Marketing Campaign Recommendations

6 Strategic Domains

1. Brand Awareness & Association Building

Current state: Hana Bank scores "no particular image" and "feels old" repeatedly, and ranks below KB, Shinhan, Woori, and Toss in top-3 preference mentions.

Recommendation: Consolidate key visuals, spokesperson, and concept — then run short, repeated exposures across digital channels (YouTube, SNS, display). Build a single message around already-recalled elements (Son Heung-min × forex, G-Dragon visual). Consider e-sports, gaming, and youth culture sponsorships and collabs to strengthen "young brand" association.

2. App (Hana Q) Experience-Linked Campaign

Current state: Hana Q is seen as "clean and secure" but faces "slow loading," "stability issues," and "users don't get information from this app."

Recommendation: Provide actionable in-app information (investment, savings, spending summaries) via in-app, push, and banners — to shift perception to "I get useful info from this app." Introduce edu/experience-sharing campaigns highlighting security and spending tracker strengths via digital creators and short-form content. After performance improvements, run a "faster and more stable" re-launch campaign.

3. Channel & Content Strategy (Tailored to MZ Financial Interest)

Current state: MZ consumers have high interest in investing and basic finance, getting information from Naver news, banking apps, YouTube finance channels, group chats, books, and courses.

Recommendation: Partner with YouTube/podcast finance influencers to create practical content series (basic finance, account splits, fixed deposits, global investing) with Hana Q/Hana Bank credits. Design UGC-friendly events/campaigns ("today's spending summary," "monthly savings check") for group chats, communities, and in-app. Run practical information landing pages for "deposit rates, parking accounts, overseas remittance" search intent.

4. Conversion (Targeting Users of Other Banks)

Current state: Switching intent temperature is "lazy, but would switch for the right offer" — with "short-term deposit rates" and "long-term customer rewards" as cited triggers.

Recommendation: Create digital-exclusive offers with clear deposit/savings rates and sign-up benefits, leading directly to CTA (sign up/app download) in display, SNS, search, and install campaigns. Use "the longer you stay, the better it gets" creator/card news content to reinforce "no downside to switching" messaging. Test segment-specific messages/channels (rate-sensitive 30s vs. app-convenience-sensitive 20s/40s).

5. Experience-Based Memory (Expanding on the Hana Card Case)

Current state: Hana Bank main brand ads are forgettable, but the Hana Card travel popup event (airport concept, passport ticket) is vividly remembered through SNS and direct participation.

Recommendation: Design offline/popup/event "participation + experience" campaigns for Hana Bank and Hana Q, with digital amplification (SNS, influencers, Shorts). Commit to 1–2 visually distinctive seasonal campaign concepts repeated consistently across TV, online, and in-app to anchor "Hana = [concept]" in memory. Distribute social responsibility and youth culture content regularly as digital stories and video.

6. KPI & Measurement Framework

Brand: Track Hana Bank/Hana Q unprompted awareness and preference over time; measure shifts in keyword associations ("young," "trustworthy," "convenient"). Campaign: App downloads, account openings, deposit conversions per channel; video completion, clicks, and shares linked to brand survey data. Segments: Separate tracking by age/gender/employment type for exposure, conversion, and surveys to optimize messages and channels per segment.

One-sentence summary: Hana Bank and Hana Q can break the "no image / feels old" perception through a unified message and key visual with repeated digital exposure, practical in-app information with security/spending tracker strengths highlighted, MZ financial information channel integration (YouTube, influencers, search), deposit/savings/long-term-customer offers, and experience-based memorable events — designing a high-impact MZ-targeted digital campaign.
