Civic Participation in Local Government Events

Mapping awareness channels, participation triggers, satisfaction, and revisit intent — with campaign recommendations for local governments

Case Study Sections

Project Overview

An in-depth study of citizens' experiences attending festivals and events organized by local governments. The research identified awareness channels, decision-making triggers, post-attendance satisfaction, and revisit intentions — delivering qualitative insights for promotion strategy, event planning, and information design.

report_problem

Research Objective

Identify how citizens discover events, what triggers the decision to attend, what drives satisfaction or dissatisfaction, revisit intent, and key implications for advertising and promotion strategy.

person

Participant Criteria

Adults who had attended at least one local government-organized event or festival, recently or in the past. Total of 7 participants (Interviews 1–7).

celebration

Events Attended (per interview)

Bukpyeong Folk Festival, Bupyeong Folk Festival & Ttukseom K-pop Drone Show, Gumi Ramen Festival, Gongju Chestnut Festival, Goyang Flower Expo, Yeouido Fireworks Festival, Hwacheon Trout Fishing Festival, Goyang Lake Park Events, and others.

Research Methodology

smart_toy

AI Interview (Proby)

Participants were interviewed 1-on-1 through Proby's conversational AI interview platform. After an honorarium was provided, the AI collected candid accounts of event experiences, awareness channels, attendance triggers, satisfaction, dissatisfaction, revisit intent, and participants' own reflections on the AI interview experience itself.

Participants noted both the comfort of answering without pressure and areas for improvement, such as response delays and occasional speech-recognition errors.

Key Findings

"I saw a promotional video and got interested, then searched on Google, Naver, and YouTube. It was a festival well-known for fair prices and high-quality food, and all the positive reviews and ratings made me want to go."

— Interview 2

1. Awareness Channels: Multiple Channels, Compound Discovery Paths

Channel mentions: Naver/Google (3), YouTube (2), Instagram (2), word-of-mouth (2), banners/posters/flyers (1), TV/bus ads (1), news (1), SNS (1). A recurring pattern was promotional video on YouTube/Instagram → search + review check. For local residents, repeated passive exposure via streetside banners, posters, and community center notices combined with habitual "it's always around this time of year" awareness led to actual attendance.

"I've lived in Bupyeong a long time, and I saw a lot of banners and posters around the neighborhood." (Interview 1)

2. "Why Attend?" Triggers: Value, Reviews, Unique Experiences, Interest & Exposure Volume

Key triggers identified: price-to-quality ratio (fair prices, well-reviewed, no overcharging), visual and experiential novelty (giant brazier chestnuts, fireworks footage, flowers and scenery), peer reviews, topic alignment (flowers, ramen, fishing, traditional culture), regional identity, promotional density (more visible banners/posters = higher perceived trust), and exclusive activities only available at that event.

"I want to spend the weekend differently — things feel a bit predictable these days. I happened to see a promotional video and the date worked out." (Interview 2)

3. Post-Attendance Satisfaction: Value, Experience Variety, Met Expectations & Traditional Culture Feeling "Hip"

Satisfaction factors included: value for money (affordable prices amid rising costs, shareable menu items), variety of activities (ramen cooking contests, noodle challenge, flowers, trout fishing/cooking, folk street parades), real-world experience matching promotional expectations, improved convenience through QR/kiosk payments responding to prior feedback, and atmosphere — traditional culture described as "hip and approachable."

"There was a 'half-ramen' menu where you get half a bowl — we ordered different things and shared. It was a lot of fun." (Interview 2)

4. Dissatisfaction: Expectation Mismatch, Information Gaps, Theme Inconsistency & Crowding

Expectation mismatch: Last-minute cancellation of key attractions (e.g., drone show) without prior notice caused significant disappointment. Information gaps: Food and facilities were poorly communicated compared to headlining acts. Theme inconsistency: Weekly rotating themes left some attendees watching a different show than they expected. Crowding: Some gave conditional revisit intent — "I'll go back if crowd management improves."

"A drone show was advertised, but it got cancelled on the day. I had no idea cancellation was even possible, so I was very disappointed." (Interview 1)

5. Revisit Intent: Mostly Positive, Conditional on Quality Maintenance

Most of the 7 participants expressed positive revisit intent: "I want to go again," "planning to bring a friend next year." Conditions included maintaining quality and convenience, improving crowd management, and finding the event sufficiently appealing. Continuous improvement based on feedback and attending with a companion were both cited as revisit motivators.

"I would go again." (Interview 2) / "I'd need it to be genuinely appealing enough to justify attending." (Interview 1)

6. Dual-Structure Promotion, "Uniqueness & Direct Experience," Transparency & Shifting Perceptions of Tradition

Promotion = attract attention (visual) + build trust (reviews + real info): Initial awareness often comes through YouTube/Instagram visuals, but promotional videos were noted as separate from actual event information. At the decision stage, reviews and peer feedback played a critical role. "Uniqueness" and "direct experience" were central motivators and satisfaction drivers. Information transparency and expectation management were key. Notably, one participant described a meaningful perception shift: "traditional culture felt distant, but experiencing it locally made it feel hip and relatable."

"The promotional video and the actual festival are two different things." (Interview 2)

Campaign Recommendations

Channel, Messaging & Information Design

Channel: Design Separate "Awareness" and "Validation" Touchpoints

Use YouTube/Instagram for short, visually striking attention-grabbers. Ensure Naver/Google search results surface reviews, pricing, menus, and program summaries. Link detailed information directly from the same campaign creative.

Message: Name the Exclusive Experience

Highlight specific experiential hooks (e.g., "roast chestnuts in a giant brazier," "share multiple ramen varieties with the half-bowl menu"). If there are convenience improvements from prior feedback, include a brief "we listened" story — e.g., "Updated based on last year's feedback."

Information: Expectation Management & On-Site Needs

Proactively communicate the possibility of cancellation or changes to key programs, along with backup options. Add a "quick overview" format to promotional materials and social posts covering food, facilities, and per-theme schedules.

Local Offline + Online Integration

Distribute banners and posters along main thoroughfares, community centers, and daily commute routes. Unify QR codes, hashtags, and search terms across all materials to guide audiences toward reviews and detailed information.

Target & Tone

Target "looking for a different kind of weekend" and "great with friends or family." Tone: "Unique but approachable and affordable — better with company." For traditional and local culture, frame as "something that felt distant, now close enough to enjoy."

This case study is based on a synthesis of 7 AI interviews (Interviews 1–7) conducted for local government event participation research.
